# Fact-check & sources — *The Psychological Trick That Keeps You Obedient*

- **Channel:** Barry's Economics
- **Video:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ApSH0fCIjTY
- **Companion to:** `Barry's Economics - The Psychological Trick That Keeps you Obedient [ApSH0fCIjTY].en-orig.md`

## Method

Every checkable factual, historical, and scientific claim in the transcript was extracted and verified twice: once by hand against live web sources, and once by an independent multi-agent workflow (each claim researched, then adversarially re-checked by a second agent). The two passes agreed on all 24 verdicts. Where the passes disagreed in emphasis, the stronger primary source was used.

## Bottom line

For a comedy channel, this is exceptionally well researched, and the central argument rests on solid ground. Every named authority, study, and theory is real and correctly attributed, and the academic citations are essentially flawless. The weaknesses are confined to specific numbers and rhetorical scope — a handful of statistics are reversed, inflated, or quietly carried over from the Victorian era or from a different unit (annual vs. cumulative). Nothing is fabricated. Almost all of the simplifications are ones the presenter visibly signals as generalizations.

Verdict key: ✅ True · ✅ Mostly true (minor caveat) · ⚠️ Mixed (partly right, partly not) · ❗ Misleading (kernel of truth, but the specific claim is wrong as stated).

## The claims that need correcting

### ❗ "Female servants dismissed from service accounted for 50% of prostitutes in Edwardian England."

The video's most striking statistic is **directionally reversed and misdated**. The real figure traces to historian Judith Walkowitz, *Prostitution and Victorian Society* (Cambridge University Press, 1980), p. 16: of prostitutes recorded in **Millbank prison**, more than 90% were daughters of unskilled or semi-skilled working men, of whom **more than 50% had previously been servants** (the rest having worked as laundresses, charwomen, street sellers, etc.). So the genuine statistic is "roughly half of (one prison sample of) prostitutes had previously been servants" — not "dismissed servants made up 50% of all prostitutes." It is also **Victorian**, not Edwardian, and "dismissed" is the video's framing (the data record prior *occupation*, not how the women left service). The defensible kernel: domestic service was the single largest documented prior occupation of recorded prostitutes.

- [Contagious Diseases Acts — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contagious_Diseases_Acts) (carries the verbatim Walkowitz quote with footnote to p. 16)
- [Walkowitz, *Prostitution and Victorian Society* — Cambridge University Press](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/prostitution-and-victorian-society/0D62DC0E48D49A3FA0630DBAD4DCB35B)
- [Victorian Prostitution — British Literature Wiki, University of Delaware](https://sites.udel.edu/britlitwiki/victorian-prostitution/)

### ❗ "Unelected lords voting down every single piece of social reform for a century."

The *causal chain* is accurate: a Conservative-dominated, unelected House of Lords (only ~88 of 602 members were Liberals) rejected the 1909 People's Budget, triggering the constitutional crisis that produced the **Parliament Act 1911**, which stripped the Lords' veto. But "**every single piece**" is false — the Lords let the **Trade Disputes Act 1906** pass, and the **Old Age Pensions Act 1908** could not be blocked at all (it was treated as a money bill). They were selective, targeting bills such as Education (1906) and Licensing (1908). "**For a century**" is hyperbole: the acute struggle lasted about three years, and the structural Conservative majority dates to the 1886 Liberal split.

- [UK Parliament — "Mr Balfour's poodle" (Living Heritage)](https://www.parliament.uk/about/living-heritage/evolutionofparliament/houseoflords/parliamentacts/overview/balfourspoodle/)
- [Iain McLean, "The 1909 budget and the destruction of the unwritten constitution" — History & Policy](https://historyandpolicy.org/policy-papers/papers/the-1909-budget-and-the-destruction-of-the-unwritten-constitution/)
- [Parliament Act 1911 — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parliament_Act_1911)

### ❗ "An average of 70 days before somebody grooms you… in this troop, it was an average of 4 days."

The effect is real but the **numbers are garbled**. In Sapolsky's own essay, resident females first groom newly arrived males an average of **~20 days** after arrival in the peaceful Forest Troop, versus **~78 days** in normal savanna troops (and there is "four times as much grooming of males by females" in Forest Troop). So "70" is a loose rounding of the genuine ~78-day figure, but "**4 days**" appears to be a confusion with the separate "**4× as much grooming**" multiplier. The directional point — that the peaceful troop integrates newcomers roughly four times faster — is genuinely supported.

- [Sapolsky, "Peace Among Primates" — Greater Good, UC Berkeley](https://greatergood.berkeley.edu/article/item/peace_among_primates)
- [Sapolsky & Share (2004), "A Pacific Culture among Wild Baboons," *PLoS Biology* 2(4):e106 — full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC387274/)

### ❗ "The NHS spends billions on antidepressants."

The annual spend is **hundreds of millions, not billions** — roughly **£250–285 million per year** (PharmaTimes reports a 2015 Net Ingredient Cost of £284.7M; NHSBSA data imply around £250M/year recently). The "£1.23 billion" figure that circulates is an explicit **five-year cumulative (2020–2025)**. So "billions" overstates the implied annual rate by roughly 4–5×; it is only literally true as a multi-year total.

- [Stedman et al. (2025), prescribing-cost analysis, *Human Psychopharmacology* — full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12360015/)
- [PharmaTimes (2015): "£780,000 a day," annual NIC £284.7M](https://pharmatimes.com/news/nhs_spends_780,000_a_day_on_antidepressants_1073193/)
- [YuLife / NHSBSA data: £1.23bn over five years](https://yulife.com/blog/antidepressant-prescriptions-nhs-england-2020-2025/)

### ⚠️ "When 16 million people watch Downton Abbey… one of the most watched television dramas in history."

Two sub-claims that fare differently. **"16 million" is unsupported** as a UK figure: BARB per-series averages were 9.7M–11.9M, with consolidated peaks around 12.1M (the closest large number is the US Season-4 peak weekly average of 13.3M). But "**one of the most-watched dramas**" is defensible: an estimated ~120 million global audience, rights sold in 220+ territories, and the most-watched drama on both ITV and PBS. (Note: the absolute "in history" superlative is press hyperbole; the sourced superlatives are "most-watched drama in PBS history" and "most popular series in the history of British television.")

- [Downton Abbey — Wikipedia (BARB series averages)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Downton_Abbey)
- [Forbes (2016): 13.3M US peak, ~120M global, 220+ territories](https://www.forbes.com/sites/hayleycuccinello/2016/03/06/downton-abbey-by-the-numbers-farewell-to-a-multimillion-dollar-dynasty/)

### ⚠️ "Chronic low status physically changes the brain… depletes serotonin… makes the threat detection system hyperactive… more risk-averse."

This bundles three different things. The **cortisol** part is genuinely Sapolsky's, but it holds mainly in *stable* hierarchies and is moderated by personality, coping style, and social support. The **amygdala / hyperactive threat-detection** part is real neuroscience, but it comes from rodent work (Mitra & Sapolsky, PNAS 2008), not the baboon census. The **serotonin "depletion"** claim is direction-contested across primate studies and is not a Sapolsky finding — some studies find subordinates with *higher* CSF serotonin metabolites. The "**more risk-averse**" conclusion is an unsupported extrapolation (the stress/risk literature is genuinely mixed). Hence "Mixed."

- [Sapolsky et al. (1997), "Hypercortisolism associated with social subordinance," *Arch Gen Psychiatry* — PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9400351/)
- [Mitra & Sapolsky (2008), corticosterone, anxiety, amygdaloid dendritic hypertrophy, *PNAS* — PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18391224/)
- [Raleigh & McGuire et al. (1984), blood serotonin and social status in monkeys, *Arch Gen Psychiatry* — PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6703857/)
- [Yodyingyuad et al. (1985), CSF 5-HIAA and dominance in talapoin monkeys — PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2419791/)

## Verified accurate (with sources and small fixes)

### System justification theory (John Jost)

**The wage quote is genuine and near-verbatim.** The narration ("Over a 30-year period, the inflation-adjusted hourly wage in the US declined by more than 7% and yet the share of total income going to the top 1% nearly tripled") reads almost word-for-word from Jost, Gaucher & Stern (2015), "'The World Isn't Fair': A System Justification Perspective on Social Stratification and Inequality" (*APA Handbook of Personality and Social Psychology*, Vol. 2): *"Between 1976 and 2007… the inflation-adjusted hourly wage declined by more than 7%, whereas the share of total income going to the top 1% of the income distribution nearly tripled during the same time period (Frank, 2010)."* The top-1% share rose 8.9% → 23.5% (≈2.6×, "nearly tripled"), corroborated independently by Saez and the CBPP. The "−7%" is a real but series-specific figure that Jost inherited from economist Robert Frank.

- [Jost, Gaucher & Stern (2015) — full text (contains the exact quoted sentence)](https://irsj.ca/assets/papers/2015_jost_gaucher_stern_the_world_isn%E2%80%99t_fair_a_system_justification_perspective_on_social_stratification_and_inequality.pdf)
- [Saez, "Striking It Richer" (UC Berkeley, 2022) — top-1% share 23.5% in 2007](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~saez/saez-UStopincomes-2022.pdf)
- [CBPP — top-1% income gains (confirms the 8.9% → 23.5% series)](https://www.cbpp.org/research/top-1-percent-of-americans-reaped-two-thirds-of-income-gains-in-last-economic-expansion)

**Jost & Banaji (1994)** — "The Role of Stereotyping in System-Justification and the Production of False Consciousness," *British Journal of Social Psychology* 33:1–27. Correct attribution; the transcript misspells "Mazarin" — it is **Mahzarin** Banaji.

- [Jost & Banaji (1994) — Wiley Online Library](https://bpspsychub.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.2044-8309.1994.tb01008.x)
- [Mahzarin Banaji — Wikipedia (spelling)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahzarin_Banaji)

**"Jost at NYU in the 1990s"** is anachronistic. The theory did begin in the 1990s, but in 1994 Jost was a Yale doctoral student (PhD 1995); he was then at Stanford's Graduate School of Business and only joined **NYU in 2003**. The substance — a social psychologist began asking in the 1990s why people at the bottom of hierarchies defend them — is an accurate paraphrase of the theory's founding question.

- [John Jost — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Jost)
- [John T. Jost — AAPSS Fellows (career chronology)](https://www.aapss.org/fellows/fellow/john-jost/)

**Kay & Jost (2003), "Complementary Justice," *JPSP* 85:823–837.** Exposure to "poor but happy" / "rich but miserable" (and "poor but honest" / "rich but dishonest") exemplars raised system justification — exactly as described.

- [Complementary Justice — RePEc / Stanford GSB record (authors and finding)](https://ideas.repec.org/p/ecl/stabus/1753r.html)

**Palliative function of ideology.** The term was coined by Jost & Hunyady (2002); Napier & Jost (2008) is the well-known survey evidence ("Why are conservatives happier than liberals?"). The reduced-distress / higher-well-being effect is real in the short term; the video omits the established long-term cost — disadvantaged system-justifiers tend to suffer in self-esteem and subjective well-being over time.

- [Jost & Hunyady (2002), "The palliative function of ideology," *European Review of Social Psychology*](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10463280240000046)
- [Napier & Jost (2008), "Why Are Conservatives Happier Than Liberals?," *Psychological Science*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2008.02124.x)

### Robert Sapolsky and the baboons

**Sapolsky, the method, and the location** are all correct. He is the Stanford professor of biology, neurology, and neurosurgery; he studied wild olive baboons (*Papio anubis*) in the **Masai Mara Reserve, Kenya**, from 1978, darting them unaware with an anaesthetic blowgun syringe and measuring blood cortisol against social rank, with samples taken at a fixed morning window and excluded if taken after a fight, during illness, or after mating. (The transcript's "Masai Mara… Kenya" is correct; it is Sapolsky's *popular* writing that loosely says "Serengeti," which lies mostly in Tanzania.)

- [Sapolsky & Share (2004), *PLoS Biology* — full text (species, location, blowgun method)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC387274/)
- [Robert Sapolsky — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Sapolsky)
- ["Going Wild" — Stanford Magazine (fieldwork profile)](https://stanfordmag.org/contents/going-wild)
- [Sapolsky (1992), "Cortisol concentrations and rank instability," *Psychoneuroendocrinology* — PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1287688/)

**Low-rank physiology** is confirmed: subordinate males show elevated basal cortisol, sluggish endocrine/cardiovascular stress recovery, fewer circulating lymphocytes, suppressed **HDL cholesterol**, and higher resting blood pressure. The transcript's "suppressed HDL cortisol" is a transcription garble — HDL is "good cholesterol," a separate marker from cortisol. (The pattern holds in stable hierarchies and is moderated by personality and social support.)

- [Marmot & Sapolsky (2014), "Of Baboons and Men" — NCBI Bookshelf (verbatim marker list)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK242456/)
- [Sapolsky, Alberts & Altmann (1997), *Arch Gen Psychiatry* — PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9400351/)

**The Forest Troop tuberculosis story** is accurate. Bovine TB from a tourist-lodge garbage pit (1983–1986) killed all the refuse-eating, more-aggressive males (46% of adult males); the troop became markedly less aggressive and more affiliative, the female-to-male ratio more than doubled, and the peaceful culture persisted through a complete turnover of the male population — transmitted to incoming adolescent males, largely via the females. The colloquial "we don't do crap like that here" is Sapolsky's own informal phrasing from his talks.

- [Sapolsky & Share (2004), *PLoS Biology* 2(4):e106 — full text](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC387274/)
- [PubMed record (PMID 15094808)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15094808/)

### Edwardian history

**Poverty ~25% c. 1900** — Seebohm Rowntree's York survey (1899): **27.8%**; Charles Booth's London survey: **30.7%**. "A quarter" is a fair, conservative rounding. Caveat: these were *urban* surveys (York, London), generalized in the video to "the British population" — there was no single national poverty measure at the time.

- [Poverty in York — The Rowntree Society (27.8%)](https://www.rowntreesociety.org.uk/explore-rowntree-history/rowntree-a-z/poverty-in-york/)
- [How Charles Booth mapped London poverty — London Museum (30.7%)](https://www.londonmuseum.org.uk/collections/london-stories/how-charles-booth-mapped-london-poverty/)

**Life expectancy "around 50"** — about 47–52 around 1900–1912 (ONS English Life Tables put 1910–12 at 51.5 male / 55.4 female; some sources put 1901 male as low as ~45). "Around 50" is fair, with the important caveat that the figure is life expectancy *at birth*, heavily depressed by infant and child mortality — those who reached adulthood typically lived considerably longer.

- [English Life Tables No. 17 — ONS (1910–12: 51.5 M / 55.4 F)](https://cy.ons.gov.uk/peoplepopulationandcommunity/birthsdeathsandmarriages/lifeexpectancies/bulletins/englishlifetablesno17/2015-09-01)
- [A Brief History of Life Expectancy — Local Histories (childhood-mortality caveat)](https://localhistories.org/a-brief-history-of-life-expectancy/)

### Downton Abbey production and plot

**Highclere Castle** is the principal filming location ("the real Downton Abbey"), home of the Earl of Carnarvon; the downstairs/servants' scenes were built and shot at Ealing Studios. **Julian Fellowes** is the creator, sole writer, and executive producer; he is a Conservative life peer (Baron Fellowes of West Stafford) — relevant to the video's point that he "didn't sit down to write propaganda" (a claim about intent, not a verifiable fact). The plot points are all accurate: **Bates** was Lord Grantham's batman in the (Second) Boer War, walks with a cane from a leg injury, and is hired as valet in episode 1 against the staff's objections; **Carson** is blackmailed by his former music-hall double-act partner Charles Grigg ("The Cheerful Charlies"), and Lord Grantham pays Grigg £20 with a threat of prosecution; **"What is a weekend?"** is a real, famous line spoken by the Dowager Countess (Maggie Smith).

### Marx and US inequality

**Marx** — "Religion… is the opium of the people," from *A Contribution to the Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right* (written 1843, published 1844). "Opiate of the masses" is the common later paraphrase. The "opium was the everyday medicine of the day" framing is historically sound — laudanum was the era's ubiquitous over-the-counter painkiller — which supports the "palliative" reading. (Comparing it to ibuprofen understates opium's potency and addictiveness, but the analogy holds for its role as the common analgesic.)

- [Marx, *Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right*, Introduction — Marxists Internet Archive](https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1843/critique-hpr/intro.htm)
- [Opium of the people — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_of_the_people)

**US inequality over ~30 years** — well supported. CBO: top-1% real after-tax income rose ~262% (1979–2019). Piketty–Saez–Zucman: the bottom-50% share of national income fell from ~20% to 12.5% (1980–2014). EPI: top-1% wages +182% vs bottom-90% +44% since 1979. The "stayed the same or got worse" framing is literally accurate for real *wages* (roughly flat for the median worker over ~40 years), though it understates modest gains in real median *household* income.

- [CBO — The Distribution of Household Income, 2019](https://www.cbo.gov/publication/58781)
- [Piketty, Saez & Zucman (2018), *Quarterly Journal of Economics*](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/133/2/553/4430651)
- [Pew Research — real wages have barely budged for decades (2018)](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2018/08/07/for-most-us-workers-real-wages-have-barely-budged-for-decades/)
